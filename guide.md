## Core Principles

Recruiters spend **15–30 seconds** per resume. Every word must earn its place.

| Principle                | Why It Matters                                    |
| ------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------- |
| **Conciseness**          | Recruiters skim — dense paragraphs get skipped    |
| **Relevance**            | Irrelevant details dilute strong accomplishments  |
| **Evidence over claims** | "Increased revenue by 30%" beats "results-driven" |
| **Consistency**          | Inconsistent formatting signals carelessness      |
| **Active voice**         | Shows ownership and impact                        |

## Structure & Layout

### Page Count

| Experience Level | Recommended Length |
| ---------------- | ------------------ |
| < 5 years        | 1 page (strict)    |
| 5–10 years       | 1 page (preferred) |
| 10+ years        | 2 pages max        |

> Never exceed 2 pages. Anything beyond page 2 will not be read. If you use 2 pages, put your strongest achievements on page 1.

### Ideal Resume Metrics

| Metric              | Target Range                                     |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| Word count          | 400–700 (entry/mid), up to 850 (senior)          |
| Bullet points       | 12–20 in experience sections                     |
| Content in bullets  | 90%+ (avoid paragraphs)                          |
| Bullet point length | 10–30 words each, never exceeding 2 visual lines |

### Recommended Sections (in order)

1. **Name & Contact Info** — name, email, LinkedIn, city
2. **Summary** _(optional)_ — only if 10+ years experience, career change, or dense 2-page resume
3. **Professional Experience** — the core of your resume
4. **Skills** — hard skills and technical proficiencies only
5. **Education** — concise, no high school for mid/senior level

### Sections to NEVER Include

| Section              | Why                                                                                |
| -------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Objective**        | Outdated — it's obvious you're seeking a job                                       |
| **References**       | Employers will ask if they need them                                               |
| **Personal details** | Date of birth, gender, nationality, marital status — irrelevant and unprofessional |
| **Passport/SSN**     | Security risk, never include                                                       |

## Writing Powerful Bullet Points

### The Formula

```
[Strong Action Verb] + [What You Did] + [Quantified Result/Impact]
```

### Rules

1. **Start every bullet with a strong action verb** — never "Responsible for" or "Duties included"
2. **Use active voice** — the subject performs the action
3. **One accomplishment per bullet** — keep it focused
4. **Past tense for previous roles** — present or past tense is acceptable for current role
5. **No personal pronouns** — remove "I", "my", "we", "our"

### Before & After Examples

**Responsibility-oriented (weak):**

> Responsible for the coordinated management of multiple projects directed toward strategic business objectives

**Accomplishment-oriented (strong):**

> Managed a 5-member cross-functional team and coordinated with six business partners toward the successful launch of an e-commerce platform

---

**Passive voice (weak):**

> Customer satisfaction was improved by 25%

**Active voice (strong):**

> Improved customer satisfaction by 25%

---

**With pronouns (weak):**

> I led the full redesign of their website using findings from competitive research; they increased their website leads by 200%

**Without pronouns (strong):**

> Led full redesign of ABC Co.'s website using findings from competitive research; increased website leads by 200%

---

**With filler words (weak):**

> Carried out evaluations of various strategies in order to save clients $200k in fixed costs

**Without fillers (strong):**

> Evaluated 15+ strategies to save clients $200k in fixed costs

## Quantifying Your Impact

**The best resumes quantify 75%+ of their bullet points.** Hard numbers make your impact tangible.

### Types of Metrics You Can Use

| Metric Type    | Examples                                                   |
| -------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Financial**  | Revenue generated, costs saved, budget managed             |
| **Scale**      | Users served, data points analyzed, transactions processed |
| **Time**       | Hours saved per week, delivery speed improvement           |
| **Efficiency** | Percentage reduction in errors, load time, response time   |
| **Growth**     | Percentage increase in users, subscribers, conversion      |
| **Team**       | People managed, departments coordinated with               |
| **Frequency**  | Daily/weekly/monthly volume of work                        |

### "I Don't Have Numbers" — Yes You Do

Every role has something quantifiable:

- **Time savings:** "Introduced automated testing pipeline, saving 10+ hours per week in manual QA"
- **Scope:** "Built API consumed by 50,000+ monthly active users"
- **Data scale:** "Processed and analyzed 10,000+ data points to optimize pricing strategy"
- **Team context:** "Collaborated with a cross-functional team of 8 across 3 departments"

> It's okay to estimate. Most resumes use reasonable estimates. If you upgraded infrastructure and made operations faster, estimate the time saved and convert it to business value.

### For Students and Recent Graduates

- **Team size:** "Teamed with 5 people to deliver..."
- **Event scale:** "Organized a hackathon with 150+ participants"
- **Data work:** "Analyzed 1,000 survey responses to identify..."
- **Project scope:** "Built a full-stack application with 15+ features serving 200+ users"

## Industry Keywords & ATS Optimization

Most companies use Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) to screen resumes before a human sees them. To pass, your resume must contain relevant industry keywords.

### How to Find the Right Keywords

1. **Read the job description carefully** — note repeated terms, required skills, and tools
2. **Mirror the language** — if they say "CI/CD pipelines," use that exact phrase
3. **Include both acronyms and full forms** — "Search Engine Optimization (SEO)"
4. **Use standard section titles** — "Professional Experience," "Education," "Skills" (ATS looks for these)

### Common Keyword Categories

| Category            | Examples                                               |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| Technologies/Tools  | React, Python, AWS, Docker, Kubernetes                 |
| Methodologies       | Agile, Scrum, CI/CD, TDD                               |
| Concepts            | Microservices, REST APIs, system design                |
| Certifications      | AWS Certified, PMP, CPA                                |
| Soft skill evidence | Cross-functional collaboration, stakeholder management |

> Do NOT keyword-stuff. Every keyword should appear naturally within an accomplishment-driven bullet point.

## Job Fit — Tailoring Your Resume

**Job fit measures how focused your resume is on your target role.** Aim for 90%+ relevance.

### How to Improve Job Fit

1. **Remove** irrelevant bullets entirely — fewer strong bullets beat many weak ones
2. **Rewrite** bullets to emphasize skills relevant to the target role

### Rewriting Examples

**Moving from Customer Service to Data Analysis:**

> Before: "Resolved 50+ customer tickets daily and maintained high satisfaction scores"
>
> After: "Analyzed ticket resolution data to create weekly efficiency reports, identifying patterns that reduced response time by 25%"

**Moving from Teaching to Management:**

> Before: "Taught 5th grade mathematics to 30 students"
>
> After: "Strategized with school leadership to implement new curriculum, leading to 15% improvement in department performance metrics"

### The Litmus Test

For each bullet point, ask: _"Would this matter to someone hiring for my target role?"_

- **Yes** → Keep it
- **Partially** → Rewrite to emphasize relevant aspects
- **No** → Remove it or replace with something relevant

## The Skills Section

A short, optional section for hard skills and technical proficiencies that aren't covered elsewhere.

### Do

- List **hard skills, tools, and technologies**
- Categorize skills (e.g., Languages, Frameworks, Tools, Databases)
- Optionally include proficiency levels (Advanced, Experienced, Familiar)
- Keep it concise — one section, not a full page

### Don't

- List soft skills ("team player," "hard worker")
- Use visual bars, charts, or graphs to represent skill levels
- Include buzzwords or clichés ("results-driven," "detail-oriented")

### Example Format

```
Languages:     JavaScript (Advanced), Python (Experienced), Go (Familiar)
Frameworks:    React, Next.js, Express, Django
Tools:         Docker, Kubernetes, AWS, Git, CI/CD
Databases:     PostgreSQL, MongoDB, Redis, Elasticsearch
```

## Summary (Optional)

**Most resumes do NOT need a summary.** Your resume is already a summary of your accomplishments.

### When to Include One

- 10+ years of experience
- Career change (to explain the pivot)
- Dense 2-page resume (to provide a quick overview)

### When to Skip It

- Entry-level or student resumes (never include)
- Your summary just repeats your experience section
- You can't write one without resorting to buzzwords

### Summary Template (Mid/Senior Level)

```
[Job Title] with [X] years of experience in [2-3 relevant skills];
[Action Verb] + [Accomplishment] + [Metric], [Action Verb] + [Accomplishment] + [Metric].
```

**Example:**

> Software engineering lead with 10 years' experience implementing backend systems in C++; led re-architecture of key platform serving 100,000 requests/month, increasing speed by 20%; promoted twice in 18 months, six months ahead of schedule.

### Summary Rules

| Metric                  | Target      |
| ----------------------- | ----------- |
| Length                  | ≤ 100 words |
| Quantified achievements | 2+ metrics  |
| Buzzwords               | Zero        |

> A generic summary is worse than no summary at all.

## Education Section

### By Career Stage

**Entry-level / Recent Graduate:**

- Place education section **above** experience
- Include relevant coursework, GPA (if 3.5+), honors, relevant projects
- Include study abroad or relevant extracurriculars

**Mid-level (3–10 years):**

- Place education section **below** experience
- Keep it minimal: degree, school, graduation year
- Optionally include impressive honors (Dean's List, fellowships) — worth keeping for up to 10 years post-graduation
- Remove high school entirely

**Senior-level (10+ years):**

- Degree, school — that's it
- Remove dates if you prefer
- Remove all coursework, GPA, and honors

### What to Include

```
Bachelor of Science in Computer Science
University of California, Berkeley — 2018
```

Optionally add:

- Dean's List, Magna Cum Laude
- Relevant certifications (AWS, PMP, etc.)
- Continuing education (only substantial programs)

### What to Exclude

- High school (for anyone past entry-level)
- 2-week seminars or conferences
- Every online course you've ever taken

## Contact & Personal Details

### Must Include

- **Full name**
- **Professional email** (firstname.lastname@gmail.com — not biker69@gmail.com)
- **City/Location** (helps recruiters assess eligibility)
- **LinkedIn URL** (ensure it matches your resume)

### Optional

- Phone number
- Portfolio/GitHub URL (for technical roles)
- Personal website

### Never Include

- Date of birth
- Gender
- Nationality
- Marital status
- Photo (in US/UK — varies by country)
- Passport/SSN numbers
- Current work email

## Formatting & Consistency

Inconsistent formatting is one of the quickest reasons recruiters reject resumes. They review 250+ resumes per opening — careless errors signal a lack of attention to detail.

### Date Formatting

Pick ONE format and use it everywhere:

| Format                   | Example                 |
| ------------------------ | ----------------------- |
| Just years               | 2020–2023               |
| Month/Year (numeric)     | 06/2020 – 08/2023       |
| Month/Year (abbreviated) | Jun 2020 – Aug 2023     |
| Month/Year (full)        | June 2020 – August 2023 |

> Mixing "06/2020" in one section and "June 2023" in another is an instant red flag.

### Bullet Point Punctuation

- Either use periods at the end of every bullet, or none at all
- If bullets contain complete sentences, use periods on all of them
- If unsure, omit periods (more common on resumes)

### General Formatting

- Use a clean, standard font (Arial, Calibri, Garamond, Helvetica)
- Avoid narrow columns that make bullets look like paragraphs
- Maintain consistent spacing between sections
- Use **reverse chronological order** (most recent role first)
- Ensure the file is ATS-parseable (standard PDF or .docx, no fancy graphics)

### Spelling

- Proofread thoroughly — spelling errors get resumes rejected
- Pick American or British English and stick to one (analyze vs. analyse, color vs. colour)
- Have someone else review your resume before submitting

## What to Avoid

### Language Anti-Patterns

| Avoid                               | Why                           | Instead                             |
| ----------------------------------- | ----------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| "Responsible for..."                | Focuses on duties, not impact | Start with a strong action verb     |
| "Duties included..."                | Same as above                 | Describe what you accomplished      |
| "Worked on..."                      | Vague, passive                | Be specific about your contribution |
| "Assisted with..."                  | Diminishes your role          | Describe your actual contribution   |
| "Helped to..."                      | Same as above                 | Own the achievement                 |
| Personal pronouns (I, my, we)       | Unprofessional on resumes     | Omit the subject entirely           |
| Buzzwords ("team player")           | Subjective, unverifiable      | Show evidence through examples      |
| Filler words ("various," "several") | Waste space                   | Use specific numbers                |
| Passive voice                       | Hides your agency             | Use active voice                    |

### Structural Anti-Patterns

- Paragraphs instead of bullet points
- Bullets longer than 2 visual lines
- More than 2 pages
- Including an "Objective" section
- Including a "References" section
- Inconsistent date/punctuation formatting
- Fancy templates with charts, graphs, or images for skills
- Using your current work email as contact

## Demonstrating Soft Skills Through Evidence

Never claim soft skills — **prove them** with specific accomplishments.

### Leadership (Target: demonstrated in multiple bullets)

**Don't say:** "Strong leadership skills"

**Do say:** "Directed a 5-member engineering team through a 6-month platform migration, delivering 2 weeks ahead of schedule"

Evidence patterns:

- Managed/led teams (specify size)
- Mentored junior team members
- Took ownership of key projects
- Made decisions that impacted the organization
- Got promoted or given expanded responsibilities

### Communication

**Don't say:** "Excellent communication skills"

**Do say:** "Presented technical architecture proposals to C-suite stakeholders, securing $500K in infrastructure investment"

Evidence patterns:

- Presented to stakeholders or leadership
- Authored technical documentation or reports
- Facilitated workshops or training sessions
- Coordinated across departments
- Interviewed users or conducted surveys

### Drive & Initiative

**Don't say:** "Self-motivated and driven"

**Do say:** "Spearheaded development of an internal tracking system used by 125 employees, delivering 20+ new features and reducing operation time by 15%"

Evidence patterns:

- Founded or launched something new
- Identified problems proactively and solved them
- Introduced new processes or tools
- Built something from scratch
- Took on responsibilities beyond your role

### Analytical & Problem-Solving

**Don't say:** "Strong analytical skills"

**Do say:** "Analyzed $800K of monthly marketing spend data to optimize campaigns; increased conversion by 12% MoM and decreased cost per acquisition by 35%"

Evidence patterns:

- Analyzed data to drive decisions
- Improved processes or systems
- Debugged or resolved complex issues
- Designed solutions to technical challenges
- Automated manual workflows

### Teamwork

**Don't say:** "Team player"

**Do say:** "Collaborated with product, design, and backend teams to redesign the mobile onboarding flow, improving user retention by 40%"

Evidence patterns:

- Cross-functional collaboration (name the teams)
- Team size and composition
- Coaching or mentoring others
- Joint project delivery with measurable outcomes

## Showing Career Growth

Even without title changes, you can demonstrate career growth.

### Strategy 1: Split Your Job Title

If your responsibilities evolved within the same role:

```
Software Engineer, Acme Corp                    Jan 2021 – Present
  → Software Engineer – Platform (Jul 2023 – Present)
  → Software Engineer – Backend (Jan 2021 – Jun 2023)
```

### Strategy 2: Highlight Expanded Responsibilities

Add bullets that show increasing scope:

- "Expanded role to lead architecture decisions for the payment service, reducing transaction failures by 30%"
- "Promoted to technical lead after 12 months; managed 3 engineers and owned the deployment pipeline"

### Strategy 3: Show Progression Across Roles

Use increasingly senior action verbs as you move through your career:

- **Early career:** Built, Developed, Implemented, Created
- **Mid career:** Led, Designed, Architected, Optimized
- **Senior career:** Spearheaded, Directed, Pioneered, Transformed

## Final Checklist

Use this checklist before submitting any resume.

### Content

- [ ] Every bullet starts with a strong action verb
- [ ] 75%+ of bullets include quantified metrics
- [ ] No buzzwords or clichés ("team player," "results-driven," "hard worker")
- [ ] No filler words ("various," "several," "in order to")
- [ ] No personal pronouns ("I," "my," "we," "our")
- [ ] No passive voice
- [ ] No responsibility-oriented language ("Responsible for," "Duties included")
- [ ] All bullets are accomplishment-oriented, not task-oriented
- [ ] Each bullet is unique — no repetitive phrases or action verbs (max 2x per verb)
- [ ] Resume is tailored to the target role (90%+ relevance)

### Structure

- [ ] Correct page count for experience level (1 page for < 10 years)
- [ ] Word count is 400–700 (entry/mid) or up to 850 (senior)
- [ ] 12–20 bullet points in experience sections
- [ ] Each bullet is 10–30 words and ≤ 2 visual lines
- [ ] 90%+ of content is in bullet points (no paragraphs except optional summary)
- [ ] Experience listed before Education (unless recent graduate)
- [ ] Reverse chronological order within each section

### Formatting

- [ ] Consistent date format throughout
- [ ] Consistent bullet point punctuation (all periods or no periods)
- [ ] Clean, standard font (no decorative fonts)
- [ ] No visual skill bars, charts, or graphs
- [ ] Proper use of white space — not too crowded, not too sparse
- [ ] ATS-friendly format (standard PDF or .docx)

### Tenses

- [ ] Past tense for all previous roles
- [ ] Present or past tense for current role (consistent within the role)

### Contact Info

- [ ] Professional email address included
- [ ] LinkedIn profile included and matches resume
- [ ] City/location included
- [ ] No unnecessary personal details (DOB, gender, nationality, photo)
- [ ] Not using current work email

### Sections

- [ ] No "Objective" section
- [ ] No "References" section
- [ ] Summary included only if genuinely needed
- [ ] Skills section focused on hard skills only
- [ ] No high school in education (for mid/senior level)

### Final Review

- [ ] Proofread for spelling errors
- [ ] Consistent American or British English throughout
- [ ] Had someone else review the resume
- [ ] Passes the "30-second skim" test — a recruiter can quickly grasp your value
